Misophonia links  to flexibility and rumination    
 
   
 2 Abstract  1 
Misophonia is a disorder involving sensitivity to certain sounds and related stimuli. Here, we 2 
explore the relationship between misophonia and affective flexibility, which describes cognitive 3 
shifting abilities in the face of emotion -evoking stimuli. Previous evidence suggests impaired 4 
subjective cognitive flexibility in misophonia, but this relationship has not been confirmed 5 
behaviourally or in emotionally -salient contexts. The secondary aim of this study is to test the 6 
potential association between misophonia and cognitive flexibility, building upon findings from 7 
previous research. The third objective is to examine the relationship between misophonia and 8 
rumination, a maladaptive cognitive process characterized by repetitive negative thinking and 9 
linked to both cognitive and affective inflexibility. One hundred and forty participants  completed 10 
the recently developed Memory and Affective Flexibility Task (MAFT), designed to assess affective 11 
flexibility, as well as a battery of self -report measures to evaluate misophonia severity, cognitive 12 
flexibility and rumination. Results suggested an inverse relationship between affective flexibility 13 
as measured by switch accuracy, but not reaction time, and misophonia severity. Cognitive 14 
flexibility was also inversely associated with misophonia severity, but notably did not attribute to 15 
task-based affective flexibility, suggesting two independent constructs both involved in 16 
misophonia manifestation. Rumination associated positively with misophonia severity and 17 
inversely with cognitive flexibility, but not affective flexibility. Taken together, these findings 18 
highlight a unique cognitive profile of misophonia, characterized by rigidity at the psychological 19 
level through cognitive inflexibility and rumination, as well as at the executive -function level in 20 
terms of affective switching difficulties.  21

 3 Introduction  1 
Misophonia is a disorder characterized by decreased tolerance to certain sounds, such as 2 
orofacial (e.g., chewing or sniffing), non -orofacial but human -based (e.g., knuckle  cracking ), or 3 
innocuous  environmental  sounds (e.g., clock ticking) . Among individuals with misophonia , these  4 
trigger sounds , and sometimes also visual (misokinesia) or other related sensory stimuli, evoke 5 
strong emotional reactions such as anger, irritation, disgust, and (anticipatory) anxiety  (Swedo et 6 
al., 2022) . These emotional reactions are accompanied by autonomic nervous system arousal 7 
(Edelstein et al., 2013; Oszczapinska et al., 2024)  and altered neural activity in areas including 8 
those related to emotion processing , the salience of sensory stimuli , and motor control  (Kumar 9 
et al., 2021; Kumar et al., 2017; Schroder et al., 2019) . Resulting behavioural  responses, such as 10 
aggression, anger outbursts and avoidance, often cause significant impairment in daily life, 11 
especially in the case of more severe misophonia  (Brout et al., 2018) . Despite prevalence 12 
estimates ranging from 4.6%  to 20% (Dixon et al., 2024; Vitoratou et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2014; 13 
Zhou et al., 2017) , research on misophonia , especially its treatment , is still at its early stages  14 
(Rosenthal et al., 2023; Swedo et al., 2022)  15 
Evidence  has highlighted the presence of executive functioning deficits in individuals with 16 
misophonia  in areas such as  cognitive  control,  response bias, emotion regulation and attention al 17 
processing  (Daniels et al., 2020; Eijsker et al., 2019; Guetta et al., 2022; Murphy et al., 2024; 18 
Simner et al., 2021) . Those affected often show a tendency to hyper -focus on certain bothersome 19 
sounds (misophonic triggers) and face significant challenges in shiftin g attention away from th ese 20 
stimuli (Edelstein et al., 2013; Jager et al., 2020) . They show selective attention deficits in 21 
response to trigger sounds as well as hypervigilant anticipation , including anticipatory 22 
anxiety  (Murphy et al., 2024; Sanchez & Silva, 2018) . This pattern may reflect deficits in 23 
attentional set-shifting , a core feature of cognitive inflexibility , which is defined as the inability to 24 
disengage  from irrelevant stimuli and redirect attention to information relevant to the task or 25 
situation at hand (Horning & Davis, 2012; Uddin, 2021) . In support of this idea, there is evidence 26 
of higher self -reported cognitive inflexibility among those with misophonia (Simner et al., 2021) . 27 
Cognitive flexibility, which facilitates  efficient shift ing between mental processes to 28 
produce contextually appropriate behavioural  responses, follows a protracted developmental 29 
trajectory and is frequently impaired in numerous common neurodevelopmental and psychiatric 30 
disorders  (Dajani & Uddin, 2015; Morris & Mansell, 2018) , includ ing obsessive -compulsive 31 
disorder (OCD ) (Gruner & Pittenger, 2017) , anorexia nervosa (AN ) (Miles et al., 2020) , autism 32 
spectrum disorder (ASD ) (Albein -Urios et al., 2018; Lage et al., 2023) , Post -Traumatic Stress 33 
Disorder (PTSD) (Popescu et al., 2023) , and a ttention -deficit and hyperactivity disorder ( ADHD ) 34 
(Zhang et al., 2023) . Misophonia shows substantive comorbidity with these conditions, and the 35 
presence of such comorbidities relate to severity of misophonia as well  (Andermane, Bauer, 36 
Simner, et al., 2023; Erfanian et al., 2019; Kluckow et al., 2014; Rinaldi et al., 2023; Rouw & 37 
Erfanian, 2018; Wu et al., 2014) . The occurrence  of these symptoms  suggests potential overlap 38 
of neurobiological or psychological mechanisms , and cognitive flexibility is one such candidate 39 
mechanism.  40 
It remains , however , unclear whether cognitive inflexibility in misophonia manifests 41 
primarily in the face of triggers vs. across a broad range of contexts. There are some indications 42

attentional bias that directs greater focus toward negative information than positive information  9 
(Shafran et al., 2002) . In the context of misophonia, this translates to an intensified focus on 10 
negatively perceived sounds  (Simner et al., 2021) . These findings may reflect cognitive inflexibility 11 
among individuals with misophonia that extends beyond contexts specifically associated with 12 
triggering stimuli.  13 
Task -Based correlates of cognitive flexibility in misophonia  14 
To understand cognitive flexibility in misophonia, it is crucial to examine whether  deficits 15 
shown on self -report data can also be reflected in a neurocognitive task. Self-report data may not 16 
accurately capture deficits in executive  functioning as they tend to emphasize personality -level 17 
traits of flexibility rather than task -specific performance  (Buchanan, 2016) . In this respect, 18 
behavioural studies have yet to establish a consistent link between task -based cognitive flexibility 19 
and misophonia . For instance,  despite reduced cognitive flexibility reported  in questionnaire s 20 
(Simner et al., 2021) , individuals  with misophonia  do not appear to perform differently on the 21 
Wisconsin  Card Sorting Task  (WCST)  (Abramovitch et al., 2023) , a task in which perseverative 22 
responses/perseverative errors are often considered to reflect cognitive inflexibility  (Miles et al., 23 
2021) . This discrepancy may stem from inconsistent definitions of cognitive inflexibility, as well as 24 
methodological and reporting biases, which likely underlie  lack of connection  between self - 25 
reported measures of cognitive flexibility and performance on neuropsychological tasks . In 26 
addition, self -report measures  may  not be reliable  proxies for neuropsychological tasks of 27 
cognitive flexibility  (Howlett et al., 2021) . As such ,  the self -reported cognitive flexibility among 28 
those with miso phonia (Simner et al., 2021)  may not inform  the extent  to which  individuals with 29 
misophonia show impairments in set shifting  or task switching , core components  intricately linked 30 
to cognitive flexibility (Dajani & Uddin, 2015; Stemme et al., 2007) .  31

Another  possible reason for the previous null findings regarding task -based cognitive 34 
flexibility in individuals affected by misophonia, would be that cognitive inflexibility is evoked only 35 
in the presence  of triggers  (e.g.,  orofacial sounds)  or other relevant stimuli . For instance, the 36 
association between impaired cognitive control and misophonia severity was specific to 37 
performance  on the Stroop task in the presence of misophonia trigger  sounds , with misophonia 38 
severity not relating to Stroop effect when faced with sounds considered to be generally 39

may only be present in certain contexts among individuals  with misophonia .  2 
Further, t he WCST contains only neutral stimuli , which we hypothesize may not be 3 
sufficient to evoke impairments in shifting or switching abilities in misophonic people.  4 
Considering the role of emotion regulation deficit s in misophonia (Dixon et al., 2024; Guetta et 5 
al., 2022) , emotionally  evocative stimuli could be necessary to evoke significant inflexibility in 6 
behavioural  responding during a task by individuals with misophonia.   The potential importance 7 
of affective stimuli to evoking flexibility deficits can be shown by drawing parallels with previous 8 
research in ASD, which has shown a relationship with misophonia in the form of heightened 9 
presence of autistic traits  (Rinaldi et al., 2023) . Task-switching paradigms revealed that switch 10 
costs associated with autism were specific to trials involving switching attention either to or away 11 
from emotional stimuli, with directional findings referring to variations in these costs based on 12 
the direction of the shift. In other words, these costs differ by direction, reflecting distinct 13 
cognitive processes engaged by emotional salience (De Vries & Geurts, 2012; Latinus et al., 2019) . 14 
Cognitive  flexibility in response to emotion -evoking stimuli is referred to as affective  flexibility 15 
(Eckart et al., 2021) . Hence i n the present study, we use a novel neurocognitive task, the Memory 16 
and Affective Flexibility Task (Allen et al., Under review)  that incorporates neutral and 17 
emotionally -arousing  stimuli in working memory and affective flexibility trials  (Lang & Bradley, 18 
2007) .  19 
Rumination: A key process related to inflexibility potentially involved in 20 
misophonia  21 
Rumination is recognized as a transdiagnostic process associated with cognitive and 22 
affective inflexibility (Altamirano et al., 2010; Davis & Nolen -Hoeksema, 2000; Genet et al., 2013; 23 
Morris & Mansell, 2018) . Defined as repetitive, self -focused thought related to  negative emotions 24 
and their origins, rumination is pervasive across a range of psychiatric disorders  (Treynor et al., 25 
2003) . Inherently rigid and maladaptive, r umination involves cycling through the same negative 26 
thoughts  without transitioning to more adaptive cognitive approaches . Inflexibility may manifest 27 
in rumination through  deficits  in the ability to di sengage  from negative emotional elements of 28 
stimuli , resulting in  difficulty shifting focus from negative to neutral or positive cognitions (Genet 29 
et al., 2013; Koster et al., 2011) . In misophonia, rumination may manifest as inflexible, repetitive 30 
negative thinking, not only about trigger stimuli and their sources but also involving internally 31 
focused thoughts, such as judgments about the misophonic reaction itself. Misophonic 32 
individuals  tend to  ruminate even when no triggers are present, such as by anticipating what 33 
trigger sounds may come next or replaying previous triggering situations (Vitoratou et al., 2021) . 34 
Although people with misophonia do anecdotally report rumination surrounding their 35 
misophonic experience , it remains unclear to what extent ruminati ve thinking  is pervasive in 36 
broader contexts as an overarching cognitive tendency  in people with misophonia . Thus, In the 37 
current investigation, we ask whether  this tendency to ruminate extends more generally to daily 38 
life, both about misophonia and other unrelated topics. We aim to explore rumination as a 39 
potential manifestation of cognitive and affective inflexibility in misophonia, focusing here on 40 
three subtypes: perseverative thinking (Ehring et al., 2011) , brooding (Treynor et al., 2003) , and 41 
anger rumination (Sukhodolsky et al., 2001) .  42

 6 Current study  1 
By employing a neurocognitive task (Allen et al., Under review) , the first aim is to evaluate 2 
the relationship between affective flexibility performance and the severity of misophonia 3 
symptoms . The MAFT  is designed to  assess  affective flexibility , which may be relevant to 4 
individuals with misophonia  as well as broader psychopathology (Friedman & Banich, 2019) . We 5 
predict an  association of self -reported misophonia severity with affective inflexibility, as indexed 6 
by worse performance on the MAFT switch trials in terms of reduced accuracy  and slower 7 
Reaction T ime (RT) (H1).   8 
In addition, we examine the relationship between self -reported cognitive inflexibility and 9 
the severity of misophonia , expecting to replicate the findings of Simner et al.  (H2) and we predict 10 
a positive association between diminished cognitive flexibility and the severity of misophonia 11 
symptoms . Furthermore, we evaluate the association between self -reported cognitive inflexibility 12 
and task -based indices of affective flexibility, specifically switch accuracy and switch RT , derived 13 
from the MAFT  to examine whether self -report flexibility aligns with task -based measures . We 14 
hypothesize that self -reported and task -based measures of flexibility w ill be positively correlated, 15 
showing  a convergence between cognitive and affective dimensions of flexibility  (H3). This 16 
investigation aims to deepen understanding of the interplay between subjective and objective 17 
facets of flexibility and their relevance to misophonia severity.  18 
Additionally , we explore the association  between rumination , misophonia  severity , 19 
affective  flexibility , given  affective  inflexibility , specifically the difficulty in disengaging from 20 
processing the emotional aspect  of negative stimuli , has been linked to increased rumination in 21 
daily life  (Genet et al., 2013) . In particular , we  expect to find a positive relationship between 22 
increased self -reported rumination , and misophonia severity  as well as worse switch accuracy 23 
and switch RT to correspond with greater levels of rumination  (H4). As part of our exploratory 24 
analyses, we also examine the relationship between cognitive flexibility and measures of 25 
rumination. We hypothesize that decreased cognitive flexibility will be positively correlated with 26 
increased levels of rumination  (H5) . 27

In the MAFT , participants engaged in two types of trials: memory trials and emotional 9 
switch trials. Memory trials required participants to determine whether the current image 10 
matched one shown N trials earlier, measuring working memory. Emotional switch trials, 11 
randomly interspersed within the task, required participants to rapidly evaluate whether an 12 
image was positive or negative, measuring affective flexibility. The working memory trials were 13 
based on the widely used N-back task (Owen et al., 2005) , designed to measure behavioural 14 
responses to emotional and non -emotional stimuli. Switch trials assessed affective flexibility, as 15 
they required participants to shift from a memory -based response to evaluating emotional 16 
valence, reflecting task switching in the context of affective stimuli. For the current study, only 17 
switch accuracy and switch RT were of interest (Figure 2) . 18 
The MAFT consisted of 198 trials organized into nine experimental blocks, with 19 
participants completing three valence -specific blocks (positive, negative, neutral) for each level 20 
of N-back difficulty ( N = 1, 2, and 3). Each block included 20 + N trials to account for initial 21 
‚Äúmismatch‚Äù stimuli before the first potential ‚Äúmatch‚Äù target. Prior to the experimental blocks, 22 
participants completed two practice blocks with 500 ms trial -level feedback, repeating them until 23 
they achieved at least 70% accuracy on N-back trials at N = 1 (11 trials) and N = 2 (12 trials). 24 
Approximately 60% of trials within each block were N-back trials (12 + N per block), consisting of 25 
four ‚Äúmatch‚Äù trials and the remainder as ‚Äúmismatch‚Äù trials, with emotional valences congruent 26 
with the block type. The remaining 40% (~8 trials per block) were switch trials, which alternated 27 
between emotional valences and were presented in a pseudo -randomized order. Stimuli were 28

Our key independent variables included MAFT switch accuracy and switch RT indices, 2 
along measures of cognitive flexibility (DFlex) , perseverative thinking (PTQ), brooding (RRS -B), and 3 
anger rumination (ARS) . The dependent variables consisted of the S -Five total score and its five 4

The rationale behind controlling for hyperacusis as confounding variable was driven by the 7 
heightened SSSQ scores, suggestive of hyperacusis, in participants with significant misophonia. 8 
This pattern persisted even after excluding the misophonia -specific item (item 4). This is 9 
consistent with previous findings of heightened comorbidity with audiological disorders like 10 
tinnitus and hyperacusis (Aazh, Erfanian, et al., 2022) . However, misophonia can also occur 11 
without audiological abnormalities (Swedo et al., 2022) . Thus, where warranted, we include 12 
additional tests for all primary hypotheses with the SSSQ as a covariate in order to control for 13 
hyperacusis.  14 
Evidence suggests that there is a high prevalence of depression and anxiety in individuals 15 
with misophonia (Aazh, Erfanian, et al., 2022; Danesh & Aazh, 2020; Erfanian et al., 2018; Erfanian 16 
et al., 2019; Erfanian & Rouw, 2018; Jager et al., 2020; McKay et al., 2018; Rouw & Erfanian, 2018; 17 
Siepsiak et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2014) . Hence, we also control for depression and anxiety by 18 
incorporating the SAD -T score as a covariate in each regression model . 19

Discussion  9 
In the current work, we extend previous research on cognitive inflexibility in mis ophonia  10 
in several ways.  First, we conjointly considered a self -report measure of broad cognitive flexibility, 11 
together with a behavioural  measure of affective flexibility.  Second, we considered how 12 
misophonia and cognitive inflexibility related to multiple forms of rumination .  Third, throughout 13 
our analyses, we considered multiple dimensions of misophonia symptoms.  Consistent with 14 
hypotheses, we  demonstrate d that affective flexibility, limited to  switch accuracy, was inversely  15 
associated with misophonia  severity and internalization , impact and outburst  factors . We also 16 
observed that cognitive inflexibility was significantly associated with  the severity of misophonia 17 
and these findings generalized across misophonia subscales , except for  internalization.  Consistent 18 
with previous work, we found that cognitive flexibility and affective flexibility were separable, 19 
with no significant correlation between these indices.  Furthermore, our findings reveal ed that 20 
various types  of rumination, including perseverative thinking , brooding,  and anger -related 21 
rumination, were  associated  with misophonia severity  and cognitive inflexibility but not affective 22 
inflexibility.   23 
 Taken together, t hese findings imply  that the mechanisms of misophonia may not be 24 
specific to sound triggers. Instead, misophonia appears to involve a complex interplay of cognitive 25 
and emotional processes, which may contribute to the heightened sensitivity and exaggerated 26 
responsivity to specific auditory stimuli. That is, we observed reduced  task-switching accuracy 27 
when faced with  emotion -evoking stimuli that were unrelated to auditory  triggers , which 28 
suggests the broader cognitive affective contributors to this condition , beyond reactions 29 
specifically linked  to misophonic triggers.  Findings also highlight the potential role of maladaptive 30 
emotional regulation strategies, such as rumination, in the path ology of misophonia . Overall, our 31 
findings emphasize  the need to consider cognitive and affective processes in understanding this 32 
condition.  33

To provide evidence of affective flexibility  deficits in individuals with misophonia , we 36 
employed a  newly developed task designed to capture affective  switching . This task allowed us 37 
to explore the relationship between two performance indices , switch accuracy and switch RT , and 38 
misophonia severity.  As expected, overall misophonia severity  and affective flexibility , limited to  39

 24 switch accuracy , were inversely associated . RT performance was not related to misophonia, and 1 
this is consistent with early evidence that RTs on this task are not specific to affective flexibility 2 
but tend to reflect a more  general motor response rate across conditions (Allen et al., Under 3 
review) . The diminished affective flexibility may hamper the ability to effectively  switch  4 
responding  to demands  in the face of emotional stimuli . This finding is in line with previous 5 
studies which highlight the role of emotion regulation in misophonia (Bagrowska et al., 2022; 6 
Barahmand et al., 2023; Cassiello -Robbins et al., 2020; Dixon et al., 2024; Guetta et al., 2022) . 7 
Similar patterns have been revealed  in individuals with high autistic traits , overlapping 8 
with misophonia symptoms  (Ertuerk et al., 2023; Rinaldi et al., 2023) , who show greater switch 9 
costs  (worse accuracy) , suggesting potential links between cognitive inflexibility and the 10 
expression of autistic traits  (Zhou et al., 2024) . Two broad, mutually inclusive classes of 11 
explanation can be distinguished  to account for the increased switch costs observed in individuals 12 
with misophonia. The Task -Set Reconfiguration (TSR) Theory (Rogers & Monsell, 1995)  posits that 13 
switch costs arise from the cognitive effort required to reconfigure a new task set during switch 14 
trials, a process reliant on endogenous, top -down control mechanisms  (Zhou et al., 2024) . 15 
Evidence suggests that cognitive control, a mechanism that facilitates task reconfiguration, is 16 
impaired in individuals with misophonia when confronted with trigger sounds that are necessary 17 
for reconfiguring the task (Daniels et al., 2020) . In addition, effective cognitive control is crucial  18 
for deploying response inhibition when needed, such as restraining automatic but contextually 19 
inappropriate behaviours , like anger outbursts in response to trigger sounds. Altered response 20 
inhibition related to misophonia also has been observed using the stop -signal task, revealing a 21 
bias toward longer stop -signal delays (Eijsker et al., 2019) . According to this framework, the larger 22 
emotion switch costs observed individuals with misophonia  may reflect difficulties in 23 
reconfiguring the emotional task set.  24 
Alternatively, the Task -Set Inertia (TSI) Theory (Allport & Wylie, 1999; Meiran, 2008; 25 
Waszak et al., 2003; Wylie & Allport, 2000)  asserts that switch costs arise from interference 26 
caused by residual activation of previous task sets. From this perspective, the larger emotion 27 
switch costs in misophonic individuals  may indicate greater susceptibility to interference from 28 
prior task sets when transitioning to an emotional task.  Both task -set inertia and task -set 29 
reconfiguration are thought to jointly contribute to the increased emotion switch costs  30 
(Vandierendonck et al., 2010) . 31 
The reduced affective flexibility in misophonia can also be better understood by  the neural 32 
correlates of misophonia. Inhibition is essential for set -switching  within  affective flexibility , 33 
facilitating the shift between behaviour, emotion or cognitive  process or task sets by suppressing 34 
competing or irrelevant ones and minimizing interference during the transition  (Arbuthnott & 35 
Frank, 2000) . Elevated orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) activity has been documented  during successful, 36 
as opposed to failed, behavioural  inhibition, highlighting its role in regulatory control processes  37 
(Deng et al., 2017) . In individuals with misophonia, the OFC exhibits hyperactivation specifically 38 
in response to trigger stimuli. This heightened activity suggests the critical role of OFC in 39 
facilitating functional synchronization among brain regions, potentially influencing the 40 
misophonic ‚Äòresponse‚Äô as reflected in behavioural  task performance (Cerliani & Rouw, 2020) .  41

 25 Heightened misophonia severity links to reduced cognitive flexibility  1 
Confirming our behavioural  findings and replicating the results of (Simner et al., 2021) , 2 
individuals with more severe misophonia showed  reduced cognitive flexibility. These findings 3 
impl y a broader contextual rigidity in misophonia, extending beyond specific behavioural  4 
responses.  Such rigidity has been observed in other overlapping psychiatric and 5 
neurodevelopmental disorder s (Geurts et al., 2009; Gruner & Pittenger, 2017; Miles et al., 2020; 6 
Murphy et al., 2012)  with high co-morbidity with misophonia (Erfanian et al., 2019; Erfanian & 7 
Rouw, 2018; Jager et al., 2020; Rouw & Erfanian, 2018) .  8 
Cognitive inflexibility in misophonia may account for heightened sensitivity to norm 9 
violations in uncontrollable conditions  (Banker et al., 2022) , reflecting a diminished ability to 10 
adapt to environmental deviations or unpredictability  (Miyake et al., 2000)  and to flexibly adjust 11 
cognitive strategies to manage change (Han et al., 2012) . Similar to individuals with autism, those 12 
with  cognitive inflexibility often resist change and struggle to shift their mental frameworks or 13 
expectations, leading to a rigid adherence to established norms (Lacroix et al., 2024) . 14 
Consequently, when faced with uncontrollable situations that deviate from these norms, their 15 
difficulty in adapting results in heightened sensitivity and an amplified reaction to perceived 16 
violations (Norena, 2024) . This rigidity intensifies discomfort and limits their ability to 17 
contextualize or tolerate variability  18 
Although mental rigidity, attributed to misophonia, is likely a key explanation for the 19 
tendency to over -attend or hyperfocus in response to misophonic triggers (Edelstein et al., 2013; 20 
Jager et al., 2020; Simner et al., 2021) , it may also represent an underlying pathological 21 
mechanism that contributes to broader perceptual and behavioural  deficits.  To advance the field  22 
and explore whether the behavioural  symptoms are underpinned by the rigidity or rigidity should 23 
be considered per se  as a symptom , experimental measures must evolve to reflect mechanistic 24 
models of flexibility deficits.   25 
The severity of misophonia is linked to elevated rumination tendencies  26 
Building on the robust evidence linking cognitive flexibility and rumination across various 27 
psychopathological conditions, we delved into the potential association of misophonia with three  28 
forms of rumination (Altan -Atalay et al., 2022; Davis & Nolen -Hoeksema, 2000; Genet et al., 2013; 29 
Lei et al., 2022; Miles et al., 2023; Owens & Derakshan, 2013) . All three forms of rumination were 30 
moderately associated with misophonia severity,  and t hese relationships remained significant 31 
even after controlling for depression, anxiety and hyperacusis . This finding  impl ies that the 32 
relationship between rumination and misophonia severity  cannot be explained by the presence 33 
of these comorbid symptoms. Although  rumination is a hallmark feature of depressive and 34 
anxiety disorders  (Ruscio et al., 2015) , both of which frequently co -occur with misophonia  35 
(Erfanian et al., 2018; Erfanian et al., 2019; Erfanian & Rouw, 2018; Jager et al., 2020; Rosenthal 36 
et al., 2022; Rouw & Erfanian, 2018) , our findings emphasize  the unique connection  between 37 
rumination and misophonia severity .  38 
Anecdotal reports, consistent with our findings, indicate that individuals with misophonia 39 
employ internal coping strategies. These strategies involve cognitive processing of trigger 40 
characteristics, anger rumination, and contemplation of emotional responses associated with 41 
misophonia, aimed at avoiding or escaping triggers (Dozier & Mitchell, 2023) . 42

 26 Yet, these findings should be interpreted in the context of cognitive flexibility, especially 1 
given its association with all forms of rumination within this cohort . First , the in ability to mentally 2 
switch between cognitive sets may lead to  perseveration  (Altan -Atalay et al., 2022; Beckw√© et al., 3 
2014; Nolen -Hoeksema, 2000; Watkins & Brown, 2002) . Second, s imilar to  mood disorders, the 4 
ruminati ve tendencies  observed in misophonia could  reflect disturbances in cognitive switching 5 
processes  (Piguet et al., 2010) , leading to maladaptive emotion regulation strategies that are less 6 
effective in the moment  (Barahmand et al., 2023; Cassiello -Robbins et al., 2020; Dixon et al., 2024; 7 
Erfanian et al., 2018; Guetta et al., 2022; Rinaldi et al., 2023; Spencer et al., 2023) .  8 
It is important to note that we were not able to disentangle the conjoint effects  of 9 
cognitive flexibility vs. rumination  in relation to misophonia.  All forms of  rumination  and 10 
cognitive inflexibility overlap in their relationship with  misophonia . Previous research supports 11 
the bidirectional effects of cognitive flexibility and rumination (De Lissnyder et al., 2012) . For 12 
instance, i n the context of AN, which shows  high comorbidity with misophonia , evidence suggests 13 
that eating disorder -specific rumination acts as a mediator between subjective cognitive 14 
flexibility and eating disorder symptoms  (Miles et al., 2023) .  15 
All in all, as we measured  multiple forms of ruminatio n and focused on general tendencies 16 
of responding to distress rather than misophonia -specific  experiences , our findings demonstrate  17 
that a tendency toward perseverative thought in misophonia extends beyond trigger -related 18 
situation s. Fortunately, perseverative thought such as rumination can be targeted in treatment  19 
through therap y modalities  such as CBT   (Querstret & Cropley, 2013) , which ha s shown promise 20 
in managing misophonia symptoms  (I. J. Jager et al., 2020) . 21 
Conclusion  22 
This study provides novel evidence  that the severity of misophonia is related to affective 23 
inflexibility , cognitive inflexibility, and perseverate  thinking. Affective inflexibility  was distinct to 24 
cognitive inflexibility and perseverative  thinking, as no significant association was observed with 25 
measures of these constructs . These findings provide insights into the rich set of affective  and 26 
cognitive dimensions involved in misophonia .  The profile of affective and cognitive difficulties 27 
observed  here are well -documented as transdiagnostic risk variables, consistent with the 28 
diagnostic overlap of misophonia with conditions such as ASD and OCD. By delineating these 29 
characteristics, the study contributes to reducing the risk of misdiagnosis and enhances our 30 
understanding of thee cognitive and affective processes involved in misophonia.  Importantly, 31 
this research offers practical implications for clinical practice, encouraging a more targeted 32 
approach to treatment protocols that emphasise addressing the cognitive aspects of misophonia.  33
